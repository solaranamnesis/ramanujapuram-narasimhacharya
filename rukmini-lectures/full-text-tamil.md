# Sri Navaneetham

### or

## The Rukmini Lectures for 1929.

---

---

Philosophers, professional or amateur, like others 
with a definite mental bias, are apt to think that what 
interests them ought to interest the rest of the world. 
‘This, plainly, is a mistake. Speculations about the ulti- 
mate nature of things ind men’s relation to them, are not 
the concern of the average man, though he may, on 0CCA- 
sions and under stress of great grief, indulge in such 
‘ speculation... The general run of men do not care much 
about such questions. What is more, they even pity 
others who waste time over such idle discussions having 
no bearing on the practical affairs of every-day life. This 
book is not for such men, the subject-matter being quite 
alien to them. 

There is another class of men also who, perhaps, will 
have nothing to do with the book, not because the subject- 
matter does not interest them, but because they object to 
the treatment. In their view, orthodoxy does not admit 
of room for any criticism or expression of personal opinion 
by individuals. Such freedom is anathema to them. They 
have no doubt excellent reason for dreading criticism and 
the possible unsettlement of their peace of mind. One 
need not quarrel with these’ either. 

Between these two extremes, there is a& class of 
intelligent seekers after truth, who do not distrust their 
reasoning faculty as liable to lead to hopeless error, but 
feel that the only way to overcome their perplexities is t0 
make the best use they can of their own reasoning powers 
with the help of previous human experience in the field. 
For these, any attempt at impartial exposition of vaTiOU8 
approximations to truth and.sympathetic valuation of what 
appeals to the modern mind in its present stage 18 wel- 
come help and there is no fear of such enquiring spirit 
turning away from the book with the cry “There is no- 
thing new here.” 

A wordof caution seems necessary here. In the 
nature of things a brand new philosophy of life is not to be 
expected. So far as the matter is concerned, everything 
possible appears to have been said long ago. . It is only in 
the manner of presentation that any originality can be 
shown. ‘This is no light service either, since it makes all 
the difference for the acceptance of & truth whether it is 
presented in an attractive shape or : otherwise. Gold and. 
gems may be valued independently, but fashion in jewel- 
lery changes and the same set of jewels will not appeal 
alike to grandmothers and their grand-daughters. It isa 
commonplace in human experience that values change as 
a result of changed mental outlook and difference in per- 
sonal temperament and it is very necessary that old truths 
be presented in a new setting. Any exposition which . 
merely follows well-worn methods is sheer waste of breath. 
Another condition of success is that even if the lecturer 
cannot suppress his personal predilections, he has to be 
fair-minded and emphasise what 18 really valuable in each 
system of philosophy (we have had quite enough of arid 
destructive criticism of faults). No one system is the sole 
repository of truth in such a clear manner that a person 
not accepting it in full can be called a fool or heretic 
straightaway. 

I venture to say that the book will be found valuable 
by any earnest-minded seeker after truth willing to take - 
the necessary trouble in reading it. The subject-matter 
18 not easy reading but the author has made the presetna- 
tion 88 accurate and bright as possible. .So far asl know 
this is the first time that such full account has been given 
in Tamil of non-Vedantic systems like the Tarkika and 
the Sankhya or non-Vedic systems like the Charvaka, the 
Baudha and the Jaina. Much time and labour have gone 
to the making of the book and the learned author has 
given references to original texts where he feels this neces- 
sary. The tone throughout is non-controversial—nothing 
else can be expected from the author’s creed that every 
. religion or system of philosophy has some valuable truth 
to convey (vide pages 279 et seq.) 

Lastly a word of explanation as to how I came to 
write the foreword. I have had the good fortune to come 
in contact with Pandit Narasimhacharya for over two 
years. He is singularly free from dogmatism but ready 
to make allowance for the other man’s point of view. 
Another striking feature in him isa scrupulous intellec- 
tual honesty that faces difficulties and does not pretend 
that words area good substitute for thought. 1 attended 
the original lectures (on which:the book in its present 
amplified form is based, as explained in the author’s pre- 
face) and also knew at each stage what pains he took in 
preparing the material for each chapter in the book. 1 
mention this to show that no intelligent, serious minded 
layman dipping into the book need be afraid of going back 
empty-handed. Philosophy, in India, has come to mean. 
endless discussion of the various) schools of Vedantic doc- 
trine and it is high time that people have a correct idea, of 
what, other Indian schools of thought have achieved. This 
book is & considerable piece of work from this point of 
view and I whole-heartedly recommend it to the general 
public. 1 am only sorry that it cannot be read by people 
not knowing Tamil. 

N. VIJAYARAGHAVAN, 

1 September, 1930, Accountant-General, Madras.
